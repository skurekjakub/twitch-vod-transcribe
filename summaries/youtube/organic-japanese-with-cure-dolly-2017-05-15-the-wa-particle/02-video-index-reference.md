This section provides a high-level "table of contents" of the main language skills, grammar points, and thematic elements that were the primary focus of this lesson.

### Core Grammar Concepts
- Understanding the true function of the は (wa) topic-marking particle
- Distinguishing between the topic marker は (wa) and the subject marker が (ga)
- The concept of the "zero pronoun" (invisible/implied subject) in Japanese sentences

- How context determines the actual subject of a sentence

## 2.2. Cultural & Contextual References

### Grammatical & Linguistic References

- **The wa-particle (は):** The Japanese topic-marking particle, written with the hiragana は (normally read "ha" but pronounced "wa" when used as a particle). This is one of the most fundamental and frequently used particles in Japanese, yet its true function is commonly misunderstood by learners.

- **The ga-particle (が):** The subject-marking particle in Japanese that identifies the grammatical subject (the doer or be-er) of a sentence. According to the lesson, every Japanese sentence must have a ga-marked subject, even if it is invisible.

- **The zero pronoun:** An invisible pronoun that carries grammatical information (specifically, the ga-marking) when the actual subject is implied by context and not explicitly stated. This concept was covered in a previous lesson and is essential for understanding how wa truly functions.

- **Jay Rubin-sensei:** Author of "Making Sense of Japanese: What the Textbooks Don't Tell You," cited as the source for the insight that the wa-particle never marks the grammatical subject. This book is referenced as an influential resource for understanding Japanese grammar beyond textbook explanations.

### Cultural Context

- **The "watashi wa unagi desu" joke:** A well-known joke among foreign Japanese learners that highlights the confusion caused by incomplete grammar explanations. The joke claims this sentence means "I am an eel" when literally translated, but in context (at a restaurant), it means "I will have eel" or "As for me, it's eel."

- **Restaurant ordering context:** Used as the primary example scenario to demonstrate how context determines meaning. The instructor explains that when multiple people are at a restaurant table with a waiter taking orders, the context makes it clear that the topic of discussion is what each person will eat.
