### Core Investment Principles

**On Holding Quality Companies Through Difficult Times:**
- "The reason that this portfolio has done so well, I believe, is because it's a combination of picking out really good companies and then more importantly sticking to those companies during difficult times."
- "It's easy to pick out good companies, but it's difficult to invest in them when other people are telling you they're not good companies."
- "Those decision points of just being willing to wait a little bit and hold is very important because if you get scared out of a stock, you miss all the outperformance that comes along with it when it eventually recovers."
- "The test for investors is identifying them and investing in them and then holding them when people are telling you to sell. And I always find that third part to be the most challenging part."

**On Sentiment Reversals in Quality Names:**
- "The tone can change very quickly for high-quality companies."
- "Typically good companies do recover."

**On Identifying Opportunity in Negative Sentiment:**
- "Those are in many cases the best stocks to look at, ones that investors are a little bit disgusted with, ones that the sentiment so negative that investors don't even want to consider it."

### Specific Investment Criteria

**What to Look For in Investments:**
- "When we look at the basic characteristics of what I look for in an investment, you can boil it down to having a dominant lead in their category. I like companies that are already winning, not ones that are way behind that I think can catch up. I want ones that are already in the lead."

**On Valuation Approach:**
- Use conservative DCF assumptions - price in deceleration, modest multiple expansion, terminal yields
- Require adequate returns (10-15%+) even with pessimistic scenarios
- Focus on free cash flow metrics adjusted for accounting distortions

### Risk Management Principles

**On Separating Market Risk from Fundamental Risk:**
- "Of course Amazon could sell off if the entire market does that happens but the fundamentals are going to continue upward."
- Accept market volatility; maintain conviction when fundamentals intact

**On Identifying True vs. Temporary Deterioration:**
- "Some of it is justified. And that's the problem with stock prices going down is there always is some validity to the stock going down."
- Distinguish between fixable execution issues (Chipotle portions) and structural breaks
- Monitor operational metrics (unit volumes, same-store sales, performance obligations) to assess trajectory

**On Trading Patterns vs. Fundamentals:**
- "Even though there are reasons that you could point to for Duolingo specifically, I believe a large part of the sell-off is simple trading patterns and I believe when investors have a greater appetite for risk, we'll see something different with this company."
- Recognize when stocks are selling off due to broader de-risking vs. company-specific issues
- "You can look at many similar companies that are in that speculative growth stock bucket and they'll have very similar stock patterns, very similar trading patterns over the past year."

### Key Investment Rules (Synthesized)

1. **Buy quality leaders, not catch-up stories** - Invest in companies already winning in their categories
2. **Hold through negative sentiment** - The hardest part is not selling when consensus turns bearish
3. **Separate trading patterns from fundamentals** - Recognize when sell-offs are technical vs. fundamental
4. **Use conservative DCF assumptions** - Require adequate returns even in pessimistic scenarios
5. **Monitor operational metrics** - Track leading indicators (performance obligations, unit volumes) not just price
6. **Accept volatility in growth positions** - High-growth stocks require stomach for drawdowns
7. **Research complex situations thoroughly** - Don't rush into emerging markets or complex accounting
8. **Maintain tech overweight** - Technology creates most value and warrants highest capital allocation
9. **Distinguish fixable from terminal problems** - Execution issues can be fixed; structural breaks cannot
10. **Buy the dip on quality names** - Market sell-offs in fundamentally sound companies create opportunity

### The Meta-Lesson

The overarching takeaway from this stream is captured in Carlson's framework for the Story Fund's success:

**"It's a combination of picking out really good companies and then more importantly sticking to those companies during difficult times. It's easy to pick out good companies, but it's difficult to invest in them when other people are telling you they're not good companies."**

Success requires:
1. **Analytical skill** - Identifying quality businesses with structural advantages
2. **Psychological discipline** - Holding through negative sentiment and volatility
3. **Time horizon** - Allowing fundamentals to overcome temporary sentiment

The third component - holding when consensus is bearish - is "the most challenging part" and the primary determinant of long-term outperformance.
