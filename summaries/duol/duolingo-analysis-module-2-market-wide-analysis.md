## Theme 1: The Mechanics of Growth Stock Rerating Events

### Main Thesis/Observation

High-multiple growth stocks experience violent repricing ("rerating") when investor expectations about future growth rates change, even when absolute growth remains strong. This is a structural feature of growth investing, not necessarily a fundamental business failure.

### Supporting Evidence & Logic

**The Trigger Mechanism:**
> "Anytime a highly rated stock, one that's trading at a higher multiple, has any concerns with its growth, investors sell out of the stock. They rate it much lower. And that's what we're seeing happen here. So, this is clearly a rerating event."

Carlson explains that Duolingo's 29% single-day decline wasn't triggered by declining revenues or user losses, but by Q4 bookings guidance of 22% YoY growth versus Wall Street's 24% expectation. The **2 percentage point miss** on a forward-looking metric caused a **29% immediate valuation compression**.

**The Valuation Math:**
The implicit logic: If Wall Street prices Duolingo for 24% bookings growth and management guides to 22%, the present value calculation changes dramatically because:
1. Bookings are a leading indicator (this year's bookings = next year's revenue)
2. High-multiple stocks embed assumptions of sustained high growth for many years
3. A deceleration pattern (even if growth remains positive) suggests the entire out-year model needs revision
4. DCF models are extremely sensitive to terminal growth rate assumptions

**The Distinction Between "Bad" and "Disappointing":**
All of Duolingo's reported metrics were positive:
- Subscription revenue: +45% YoY
- MAUs: +20% YoY (135M total)
- DAUs: 50.5M (up from 47.7M QoQ)
- Paid subscribers: +34% YoY (11.5M total)

Yet the stock fell 29%. This demonstrates that **rerating events are about expectation management, not absolute performance levels**.

### Implications for Traders

**Tactical Implications:**

The rerating framework suggests two distinct trading approaches:

1. **Momentum traders should exit immediately**: If you trade based on price action and relative strength, rerating events are clear sell signals because the multiple compression can persist for quarters or years.

2. **Fundamental value investors should investigate deeply**: Rerating events create potential entry points *if* the underlying business remains intact and management's strategic rationale is credible.

> "What investors do in these specific scenarios, in these exact situations where a stock like Dualingo sells off dramatically, what we do now can in many cases determine your future returns to a huge extent."

**Risk Considerations:**

The key risk is **misdiagnosing the rerating type**:
- **Temporary rerating**: Company shifts focus from monetization to growth (intentional strategy)
- **Permanent rerating**: Fundamental competitive threats or business model breakdown

Carlson's analysis hinges on believing Duolingo's is temporary, but acknowledges: 
> "If you don't believe it will [become a heavy company], then don't buy the stock."

**Behavioral Adjustments:**

Required mindset: Accept that price volatility is the *cost* of accessing potential outperformance, not a signal of investment mistake.

### Historical or Comparative Context

**The Netflix Parallel (2020-2022):**

> "Netflix ran into troubles with future expected growth. They said that they're not only not growing with subscribers, they're actually churning. They're going to lose some subscribers. They're going to lose about a million subscribers. Wall Street didn't like that. The stock price valuation was raided and dependent on continued fast future growth."

**The Netflix Decline:**
- Stock fell **75% from highs**
- Triggered by **two consecutive quarters** of subscriber disappointment
- At the trough, investors believed "growth would never return"
- Management's password-sharing crackdown thesis was ignored
- Stock has since recovered dramatically

**The Comparison:**
Both companies:
- Trade at premium multiples dependent on sustained growth
- Experienced quarters where growth *slowed* (didn't turn negative, just decelerated)
- Had management teams articulating long-term strategic pivots that Wall Street dismissed
- Saw immediate, extreme multiple compression

**The Parallel:**
> "Even though at the time, we can look at the real damage that happened to Netflix. Let's go ahead and take a look at how bad things really were when the stock price dropped 75%. We have Netflix here. We have total subscribers. The stock price dropped 75% right there. That was it. Because this quarter right there was slightly less than that one. 75% decrease in stock price. Total obliteration. All because of those two quarters."

The chart showed Netflix's subscriber count remained in the 220M+ range even during the "catastrophic" quarters - growth merely *slowed*, it didn't reverse.

**The Lesson:**
Markets overreact to growth deceleration in high-multiple stocks because:
1. Most investors use linear extrapolation (if growth is slowing now, it will keep slowing)
2. Institutional mandates force selling when stocks fall below growth thresholds
3. Momentum algorithms amplify directional moves
4. Short-term performance pressure prevents nuanced analysis

The disciplined investor's opportunity emerges when they can **correctly identify situations where deceleration is strategic and temporary rather than structural and permanent**.

## Theme 2: The "Stock Price as Information" Fallacy

### Main Thesis/Observation

Most investors commit a critical reasoning error: they let stock price movements inform their opinion of business quality rather than using independent fundamental analysis to evaluate both the business *and* whether the stock price reaction is justified.

### Supporting Evidence & Logic

**The Behavioral Pattern:**

> "I noticed something with this company in particular and that is that investors do what investors end up doing for every situation like this which I think is a mistake. They let the stock price movement inform them of their opinion on the stock. Meaning they don't look at the fundamentals. They don't look at the the growth pass and the future revenue expectations or anything like that. They don't look at the valuations. They simply look at a stock price going up or going down and they say that is what my opinion is."

**The Counterfactual Test:**

> "I believe in an alternate world. If you had this result and the stock price, for whatever reason, went up 10%. But nothing changed. Not the bookings, not the growth rate, none of these metrics, I believe that most investors would look at this company and say, 'This was a great quarter. The stock should probably be up a little bit.' And look, Wall Street is pricing it up, so it must have been a great quarter."

This thought experiment reveals the circular reasoning:
- Stock down → must be bad quarter → validates selling
- Same results, stock up → must be good quarter → validates buying

The fundamental results are *identical* in both scenarios, yet investor interpretation flips based solely on price direction.

**The Psychological Mechanism:**

The fallacy works because:
1. **Social proof**: If "smart money" is selling (evidenced by price decline), retail assumes professionals know something they don't
2. **Availability bias**: Price movements are immediately visible and emotionally salient; fundamental analysis requires work
3. **Narrative construction**: Humans are pattern-seekers who construct stories to explain price moves, then mistake the story for analysis
4. **Confirmation bias**: Once a price-based opinion forms, investors seek information confirming it

**The Evidence Against This Approach:**

Carlson provides multiple historical examples where price movements were *opposite* of business quality:

**Amazon (2022):**
> "In 2022, Amazon was trading at $80 per share. Right there, $86 per share. Now, Amazon trades at $240 per share. So, $80 to $240. What happened in 2022? Well, of course, it was because they're investing in capex for their retail growth and that was expensive and it caused the free cash flow to go down a lot. Investors in Wall Street looked at that and they said, 'Oh, no. We're not going to make as much money over the next year. We need to abandon the stock.'"

Amazon's business was *strengthening* (building fulfillment infrastructure for future growth), yet the stock collapsed because near-term FCF declined. Investors who used price as signal sold at $80. Those who analyzed fundamentals bought at $80 and rode to $240.

**Netflix (referenced earlier):**
The 75% decline occurred while subscriber base remained stable at 220M+ and content investment was positioning the company for streaming dominance.

### Implications for Traders

**Tactical Implications:**

> "If you let the stock price dictate your impressions of a company, if you let the movements and the volatility tell you how to feel about a company, then you would not have bought Netflix when it fell 75%. You would have sold out because the panic. It really looked bad according to the stock price."

**For Active Traders:**
- Use price as a *screening tool* to identify potential opportunities, not as final judgment
- When stocks fall 20%+ on earnings, that's a signal to *begin analysis*, not to assume the crowd is right
- The biggest opportunities come from correctly disagreeing with price signals

**For Long-Term Investors:**
- Develop independent analytical frameworks *before* price moves occur
- When price contradicts your analysis, systematically evaluate what changed:
  - Did fundamentals change? (requires updating thesis)
  - Did market sentiment change? (potential opportunity)
  - Was my original analysis wrong? (requires intellectual honesty)

**Risk Considerations:**

The opposite error is also dangerous: **ignoring price signals entirely**. Sometimes stocks fall because businesses genuinely deteriorate.

The discipline requires:
1. Have a fundamental thesis with specific falsifiable metrics
2. Monitor those metrics independently of price
3. When price diverges from fundamental trajectory, investigate *both* (What is the market seeing that I'm not? What am I seeing that the market isn't?)
4. Update thesis based on evidence, not price

**Behavioral Adjustments:**

> "This is a dangerous way to invest. After all, if you let the stock price dictate your impressions of a company, if you let the movements and the volatility tell you how to feel about a company, then you would not have bought Netflix when it fell 75%."

Required practices:
- **Price quarantine**: When analyzing a business, deliberately avoid looking at recent price action until completing fundamental analysis
- **Pre-mortems**: Before earnings, write down what results would cause you to sell vs. hold vs. buy more
- **Metrics-based triggers**: Define specific fundamental metrics that would invalidate your thesis (for Duolingo: sustained DAU decline, subscriber churn increase, competitive losses)

### Historical or Comparative Context

**The Jeff Bezos Amazon Example (Dot-Com Crash):**

> "Amazon's stock in a very short period of time went from $113 a share to $6 a share. Was very concerning. And shareholders were upset. Employees were nervous. Uh we had all of our employee base, their parents were all calling our employees and saying, 'Are you okay?' You know, this was the environment of great nervousness."

**Bezos's Response:**
> "But I looked at the numbers in the business and every month as the stock price went from 113 to six, the a number of customers went up every month. Our uh gross profits went up every month. Our operating expense, we were still in a loss position, but our our losses as a percentage of sales went down every month. every single business metric, new customers, customer repeat purchases, everything that we were monitoring through that entire period kept getting better."

**The Fundamental Insight:**
> "And so this that's one observation about bubbles in general. The fundamentals can be disconnected. The fundamentals of the business. And of course, as entrepreneurs, you're focused on the fundamentals of the business. The stock price is an output, an ultimate output that you actually have very little control over."

**The Benjamin Graham Framework:**
> "Benjamin Graham the great investor is famous for saying in the short term the stock market is a voting machine in the long term it's a weighing machine and so as founders and entrepreneurs and business people our job is to build a heavy company we want to build a company that when it is weighed it is a very heavy company."

**The Parallel to Duolingo:**
Carlson argues Duolingo is in an analogous situation:
- Stock price saying: "This business is worth 29% less than yesterday"
- Fundamentals saying: "All metrics improving (MAU +20%, DAU +6%, paid subs +34%, revenue +45%)"

**The Question:**
Is this a temporary disconnect (voting machine overreacting) or permanent repricing (weighing machine correctly assessing reduced long-term weight)?

**The Lesson:**
> "The goal for entrepreneurs like Lewis the CEO and founder of Dualingo is to forget about the stock price you don't have any control over that, then that's the long-term output of what you do have control over, which is the fundamentals, making a heavy company."

For investors, the lesson is parallel: Focus on whether management is successfully "building weight" (user growth, engagement, product improvement, moat expansion) rather than short-term price fluctuations.

## Theme 3: Platform Company Evolution: The "Invest for Scale, Then Monetize" Playbook

### Main Thesis/Observation

Successful platform companies follow a consistent pattern: sacrifice near-term profitability/monetization to capture market position and user base, then "turn the screws" on monetization once dominance is established. This creates extended periods of apparent underperformance that scare away most investors, but generates asymmetric returns for those who correctly identify the pattern.

### Supporting Evidence & Logic

**The Strategic Shift Carlson Identifies in Duolingo:**

> "Management basically said that their main focus has shifted. It's shifted away from turning the screws of monetization and making more revenue to now going for a big opportunity. and the CEO highlighted over and over again the opportunity that they're going for."

**CEO Luis von Ahn's Vision:**

> "There's there's a huge opportunity right now. We see a huge opportunity um over the next few years. education and the way people learn um are they're going to change fundamentally and and it's because of AI and also because of AI we see we have line of sight now to create an app that can teach really really well much better than anything that humanity has seen before as good as a as a human tutor but that is also more engaging um and if we're able to do that you know right now we have I don't know we just posted 135 million uh monthly active users if we're able to do an app that teaches just that well way much much better than we have now. We would be talking about billions of users that we have and that's what we want to shoot for here."

**The Scale Ambition:**
Von Ahn explicitly states he's going from "100+ million users" to "billions of users" - a **10-20x user base expansion goal**.

**The Trade-Off Mechanism:**

Von Ahn provides a specific example of how Duolingo deliberately chooses *not* to maximize near-term revenue:

> "If right now um uh a free user free users get 25 energy units at the beginning of the day uh and every exercise that they do spends one unit. If we were to do an experiment that decreases that from 25 to say 24, that's one fewer unit of of of energy per day. We know that would make us more money. that it just does because more people run out of energy, so more people end up, you know, wanting to pay to subscribe."

> "However, we also know that would decrease daily active users because it would frustrate some of the users. We've always had to make decisions about, you know, different judgment calls about this. What we mean is that what we decorizing user growth over monetization in this type of of judgment call. In the fictitious experiment that I just uh gave you, we would not launch that experiment going from 25 to 24 energy units, even if it meant, you know, quite a bit of bookings gains if it has uh a real hit on daily active users."

**The Strategic Logic:**

1. **Platform economics favor scale**: Network effects, data advantages, brand dominance all amplify with user base size
2. **Monetization is a "lever" you can pull later**: Converting free to paid users is a tactical decision with known playbooks
3. **User acquisition gets harder over time**: The best time to capture users is during platform transitions (in this case, AI transformation of education)
4. **Market windows close**: If competitors establish dominance while you optimize margins, you lose the long-term war while winning quarterly battles

**The Timing Rationale:**

> "The reality is that over the last couple of years um it has just become progressively clearer and clear that we are in a unique point in time particularly with education in terms of how education is going to happen in the world and also how well we can teach at Duolingo."

Von Ahn believes **this specific moment** (2024-2025) is a once-in-a-generation opportunity because:
- AI is fundamentally changing what's possible in personalized education
- Duolingo has technical capabilities (video call features, content generation) advancing rapidly
- The education market is in flux, creating opportunity for new dominant platforms

### Implications for Traders

**Tactical Implications:**

This pattern creates a **bifurcated trading opportunity**:

**For Short-Term Traders (Negative):**
- Bookings growth will likely continue decelerating for 4-8 quarters
- Wall Street will continue pressuring the stock on each earnings report
- Multiple compression may persist until revenue re-accelerates
- Options volatility will remain elevated
- **Implication**: Avoid or short until clear inflection in bookings growth

**For Long-Term Investors (Positive):**
- Accumulation period during market dislocation
- Management explicitly "showing their cards" about the strategy
- Clear falsifiable metrics to track (DAU, MAU, engagement trends)
- If strategy succeeds, rerating upward will be equally violent as current downward rerating
- **Implication**: Dollar-cost average during the "trough" period

**Risk Considerations:**

**The Strategy Could Fail:**

> "At first, Uber was focused on growth and they're highly unprofitable business. But Uber grew and grew and grew to scale. They seized the opportunity ahead of them and now they're highly profitable now that they've met scale. It was the same thing with Netflix."

But Carlson acknowledges this pattern doesn't *always* work. The key risks:

1. **Competition**: Competitors could capture the market while Duolingo optimizes for engagement
2. **Execution**: User growth could stall despite reduced monetization pressure (suggesting product-market fit issues)
3. **Timing**: The "unique moment" thesis could be wrong - maybe AI *replaces* language learning apps rather than enhancing them
4. **Monetization optionality**: Maybe the users Duolingo attracts with lower monetization pressure can't be converted later

**Behavioral Adjustments:**

**Required Patience:**

> "That's the influence, the impression that the stock price leaves on you. And I think that this is a dangerous way to invest."

Investors need **multi-year time horizons** to execute this strategy. If you:
- Need capital in <2 years
- Can't tolerate 50%+ drawdowns
- React emotionally to quarterly volatility
- Lack conviction in the fundamental thesis

...then this type of investment is inappropriate regardless of how "cheap" it looks.

**Monitoring Framework:**

Track **leading indicators of strategy success**, not lagging financial metrics:

*User Growth & Engagement:*
- DAU and MAU trends (should accelerate if strategy is working)
- DAU/MAU ratio (engagement intensity)
- Retention cohorts (are users staying longer?)

*Product Evolution:*
- Course catalog expansion
- Feature launches (video call, AI tutoring capabilities)
- User satisfaction metrics / app store ratings

*Competitive Position:*
- Market share in language learning
- Brand survey data
- Download trends vs. competitors

*Monetization Optionality:*
- Subscriber conversion rate trends (should stabilize, not collapse)
- Paying user LTV metrics
- Pricing power indicators

**Exit Triggers:**

Define conditions that would invalidate the "invest for scale" thesis:
- Sustained DAU decline for 2+ consecutive quarters
- Subscriber churn increasing (existing paid users leaving)
- Competitive losses (e.g., ChatGPT adding competing language features that gain traction)
- Management walkback of strategy (suggesting market window closed without success)

### Historical or Comparative Context

**Netflix (Long-Term Value Creation):**

> "Even smart analysts, great ones like Aswatorn said Netflix is just a a broken business model. That's what it does when a company focuses on scale and opportunity over immediate monetization. It can in many cases trick investors and analysts."

**The Netflix Trajectory:**
1. **2000s**: Burned cash building streaming library and subscriber base
2. **2010s**: Analysts said "free cash flow will never turn positive"
3. **2020**: Subscriber "saturation" fears, stock fell 75%
4. **2023-2024**: FCF positive, subscriber growth re-accelerated, stock recovered

**The Parallel:**
Duolingo is in the **early/middle** of this cycle (2010s Netflix equivalent), where the strategy looks broken to consensus but fundamentals are actually building.

**Amazon (2022 Inflection):**

> "Investors that looked at this as an opportunity of a company investing in its long-term future that's prioritizing lifetime value of customers and expansion over short-term profits made immense gains in that stock. I bought Amazon very close to $80 per share. I bought it under $100 per share over and over again."

**The Amazon Trade:**
- Stock fell to $80 as FCF collapsed from capex investment
- Fundamentals: Building fulfillment infrastructure for next decade of retail growth
- Market interpretation: "Amazon's margins will never recover"
- Reality: Infrastructure investment completed → margins expanded → stock tripled to $240

**The Lesson:**
> "I didn't let the market dictate my thoughts on the company."

**Uber, DoorDash, Spotify (Multiple Examples):**

> "All of these companies were money losing. All of them have very volatile histories. All of them have time periods where investors are super bullish or super bearish on the company. And over time, what matters is the weight of the company."

**The Common Pattern:**
1. Build dominant platform position (subsidize users, lose money)
2. Endure years of "this business model is broken" criticism
3. Achieve scale/network effects
4. Flip monetization levers
5. FCF inflection → stock re-rates upward

**The Current Question for Duolingo:**
Is it following this playbook successfully, or is it a company that will *never* achieve the scale needed to make monetization work?

**The Weighing Machine Test:**

> "Will it become a heavy company. Will it become a big, large, established, profitable company or not? If you don't believe it will, then don't buy the stock. If you believe it might, or there's a good chance of it, then it might be worth biting at."

The analysis framework:
- **Heavy**: 500M+ DAUs, dominant global education platform, strong unit economics at scale
- **Not heavy**: Stuck at 50-100M DAUs, niche language-learning app, unable to expand beyond core use case

Carlson's position: The evidence suggests Duolingo *could* become heavy, but probability <100%, justifying the small position size.

## Theme 4: Position Sizing as Risk Management Philosophy

### Main Thesis/Observation

Proper position sizing is not just about diversification - it's a quantitative expression of conviction, uncertainty assessment, and risk tolerance that determines whether you can psychologically withstand volatility long enough for thesis to play out.

### Supporting Evidence & Logic

**The Intentional Small Position:**

> "Dualingo has always been a small-sized position and the reasons for that were intentional. I intentionally put it as a smaller position because I recognize the inherent risk and volatility in the company."

**Carlson's Sizing Framework:**

Duolingo was sized at **2% of portfolio** (vs. 13% for Google, 11% for Amazon) based on:

**1. Volatility Assessment:**
> "It is a small company meaning that it has higher levels of volatility. Smaller levels of capital move the stock more either up or down. that increases the amount of volatility which in many cases increases the amount of risk because when stocks go up 50% or down 50% it can become much more difficult to hang on to that company."

**2. Business Model Maturity:**
> "Another reason why is because the company is less established than many other positions. For example, no one can reasonably argue that Dolingo has a much more proven mo and proven business model than Google, Netflix or Amazon. You just can't. Google, Amazon, and Netflix are just way more proven stocks. They're more at a steady state. They're more mature."

**3. Outcome Distribution Width:**
> "The probabilities, the outcome distribution is wider than these other companies."

**4. Growth Dependency:**
> "A much smaller market cap, higher volatility, a thesis more heavily dependent on continual high growth rates, and only a partially proven durable moat are all factors in the smaller size position for Dualingo compared to other more established companies."

**The Capital Math:**

Carlson breaks down the actual dollar exposure:
- **Total contributed to Duolingo**: $20,000
- **Total contributed to Google**: $100,000+

> "Google's over five times the size of my contribution to Dualingo. And that represents my levels of conviction in these stocks."

**The Damage Assessment:**

> "Before this company sold off, it was a 2% position. So, it went from 2%, it's down 50%, now it's at a 1% position. Meaning, the total impact on my portfolio, the NAV of my portfolio was dinged by 1% because of Dualingo's underperformance."

**Key Insight**: By sizing Duolingo at 2%, a **50% stock decline** resulted in only a **1% portfolio NAV impact**.

**The Comparative Context:**

This represents learned wisdom from painful experience:

> "One time with Netflix years ago, I had Netflix as my top position. Netflix was a stock that I was so bullish on, I couldn't even contain my my excitement about the company. I made video after video about the stock. You can go back and look at this very channel. Look at around 2020, 2021, 2022. That's where you'll see a lot of content about Netflix."

**The Netflix Lesson:**
> "Netflix began to sell off quarter after quarter, two consecutive quarters, and it sold down 75% from the highs. My top position was completely obliterated. Now, that was really stressful. That was not a fun thing to have happen."

**The Contrast:**
- **Netflix (largest position, 75% decline)**: "Pretty tragic event for my portfolio"
- **Duolingo (smallest position, 50% decline)**: "Not a tragic event. This isn't something that's really going to impact the portfolio performance over the long term."

### Implications for Traders

**Tactical Implications:**

**Position Sizing Formula (Implicit in Carlson's Approach):**

Position Size = f(Conviction, Volatility, Maturity, Outcome Uncertainty)

- **High conviction + Low volatility + High maturity + Narrow outcomes** = Large position (10-15%)
  - Examples: Google, Amazon, Microsoft
  
- **Moderate conviction + High volatility + Low maturity + Wide outcomes** = Small position (1-3%)
  - Examples: Duolingo, early-stage growth stocks

**The Psychological Dimension:**

> "So, it's not good. I'm not celebrating that the stock is down 50% and that it's a 1% hit to my portfolio. But the reason that I bring this up is because it's important to look at position sizing."

**Critical Insight**: The "correct" position size is the one that allows you to:
1. Maintain conviction during drawdowns (not panic sell)
2. Add to position if thesis strengthens and price falls (have dry powder)
3. Sleep at night (not obsessively check portfolio)

If Duolingo were 10-15% of portfolio, Carlson admits he would likely have sold during the 50% decline due to emotional pressure. At 2%, he can rationally evaluate whether fundamentals support holding/adding.

**The Action Implication:**

> "Now, having said that, I've taken in all of these risk factors and I assessed that this stock needed to be a smaller position and I sized it as a 2% position."

> "I don't like it when my stocks go down. I want all my investments to do well, including Dualingo, including even my small positions. I put the $20,000 into this stock because I want to make money with it."

**The Plan Going Forward:**
> "As long as I see that happen [metrics improving], I'm not going to sell the stock. And in fact, I may dollar cost average and buy a bit more."

Because the position is small, Carlson has **optionality**:
- If fundamentals deteriorate: Loss capped at $20K (~1% portfolio)
- If fundamentals improve: Can add capital to increase position to 3-4% as conviction grows
- If thesis plays out: Still meaningful absolute return even from small position

**Risk Considerations:**

**The Sizing Paradox:**

Small positions protect downside but limit upside. If Duolingo 10x's from here, a 2% position becomes 20% of portfolio - meaningful but not life-changing. A 10% position becoming 100% (unlikely to hold that long) would be transformative.

**The Solution**: Dynamic sizing based on:
- **Initial position**: Small due to high uncertainty
- **Add on weakness**: If fundamentals improve while price falls, incrementally increase position
- **Trim on strength**: If price runs ahead of fundamentals, reduce back to target size
- **Let winners run**: If both fundamentals AND price improve, allow position to grow

**Behavioral Adjustments:**

**Pre-Commitment Sizing Rules:**

Before buying any stock, write down:
1. Initial position size as % of portfolio
2. Maximum position size willing to hold
3. Fundamental metrics that would trigger adding
4. Fundamental metrics that would trigger selling
5. Price drawdown % that would test conviction

**Example for Duolingo (implied from Carlson's approach):**
1. Initial: 2%
2. Maximum: 5% (never more than 1/4 of largest position)
3. Add trigger: If DAU/MAU continue growing for 2+ quarters while stock stays down
4. Sell trigger: If DAU declines 2+ consecutive quarters or churn increases
5. Conviction test: 50% drawdown (which occurred - conviction held)

**The Disclosure Discipline:**

> "A lot of times when we look at other people's investments, we just see a list of holdings. But looking at how much money they have in every holding is incredibly important."

**Best Practice**: When sharing portfolios (or analyzing others'), always include:
- Position size as % of portfolio
- Cost basis and current value
- Conviction level (high/medium/low)
- Time horizon (short/medium/long)

This context allows readers to calibrate advice appropriately.

### Historical or Comparative Context

**The Professional Investor Parallel:**

While not explicitly stated, Carlson's approach mirrors Kelly Criterion position sizing used by professional investors:

**Kelly Formula**: f* = (bp - q) / b

Where:
- f* = optimal position size
- b = odds received on bet
- p = probability of winning
- q = probability of losing (1-p)

**Applied to Duolingo:**
- High uncertainty = low probability estimate = small Kelly position
- As conviction/probability increases, Kelly size increases

**The Warren Buffett Contrast:**

Buffett famously advocates concentration ("Put all your eggs in one basket and watch that basket"). But Buffett only makes this recommendation for investments where:
1. Business model is simple and unchanging
2. Competitive moat is wide and durable
3. Management is tested over decades
4. Valuation provides margin of safety

Duolingo meets **none** of these criteria currently, hence warranting smaller size despite belief in long-term potential.

**The Lesson:**

Position sizing should reflect:
- **What you know**: Conviction in thesis
- **What you don't know**: Uncertainty about outcomes
- **What you can tolerate**: Emotional capacity for volatility

Carlson sized Duolingo to match his **uncertainty level** rather than his **enthusiasm level** - a crucial distinction that saved him from catastrophic portfolio impact.
